What is a Pessary? A Gentle Guide to Non-Surgical Prolapse Support

January 2026

Living with the discomfort of pelvic organ prolapse—that feeling of heaviness, pressure, or a noticeable bulge—can be distressing and isolating. If you’re worried about your symptoms but the thought of invasive surgery feels daunting, please know there is a gentle, effective, and non-surgical path forward.

This path often involves a simple, supportive device called a pessary. It’s a discreet solution designed to comfortably manage your symptoms, helping you regain control and return to the activities you love.

Understanding the Pessary: A Simple Support System for Your Pelvic Floor

A pessary is a soft, flexible device made from medical-grade silicone. Think of it like a sports support or orthotic, but designed specifically to help your internal pelvic organs.

It is inserted into the vagina to gently lift and support organs such as the bladder, uterus, or bowel, often relieving pressure, bulging, and discomfort immediately.

What Does a Pessary Help With?

  • Pelvic organ prolapse (POP)
  • Stress urinary incontinence
  • Temporary pregnancy or postnatal support

How is a Pessary Different from Other Devices?

Unlike menstrual cups or contraceptive diaphragms, a pessary is designed purely for structural support and long-term symptom management.

Common Types of Pessaries

The Ring Pessary

Often used for mild to moderate prolapse and can sometimes be self-managed.

The Gellhorn or Cube Pessary

Used for more significant prolapse and typically managed by a healthcare professional.

Your Pessary Fitting Journey

Step 1: Assessment

A private consultation and gentle internal examination help determine the right device.

Step 2: Fitting

A trial pessary is inserted and checked while you move, cough, and squat.

Step 3: Follow-up

Regular follow-ups ensure comfort, effectiveness, and vaginal health.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I feel the pessary?

No — a correctly fitted pessary should not be noticeable.

Can it fall out?

Rarely, if properly fitted. If it does, contact your practitioner.
