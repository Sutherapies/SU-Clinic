Pelvic Organ Prolapse: Symptoms, Types, Treatment and Pessary Management

Pelvic organ prolapse is a common condition that affects many women, yet it is still something people often feel uncomfortable talking about. Women often search for terms such as prolapse symptoms, bladder prolapse, uterine prolapse, vaginal bulge, pelvic floor weakness and pessary fitting when looking for answers and support.

Many women assume that symptoms such as heaviness, pressure, bladder leakage or a vaginal bulge are simply a normal part of ageing, childbirth or menopause. However, these symptoms should not be ignored, and support is available.

Pelvic organ prolapse can affect quality of life, confidence, exercise, intimacy and day-to-day comfort. The good news is that there are many treatment options available, and surgery is not always necessary.

What is Pelvic Organ Prolapse?

Pelvic organ prolapse, sometimes called vaginal prolapse, happens when the pelvic floor muscles and supporting tissues weaken. This can cause the bladder, uterus, bowel or vaginal walls to drop down and create a feeling of heaviness, dragging or a vaginal bulge.

Many women search online for phrases such as “bladder dropping”, “feeling something coming down”, “pelvic heaviness” or “bulge in the vagina”, without realising these can all be signs of prolapse.

The pelvic organs include:

  • The bladder
  • The uterus
  • The bowel
  • The rectum
  • The top of the vagina

Prolapse can range from mild to more severe, and symptoms can vary significantly from person to person.

Common Causes and Risk Factors

There is rarely one single cause of prolapse. It usually develops over time due to a combination of factors that increase pressure on the pelvic floor.

  • Pregnancy and childbirth
  • Forceps delivery or prolonged labour
  • Menopause and hormonal changes
  • Chronic constipation or straining
  • Repeated heavy lifting
  • Persistent coughing
  • High-impact exercise
  • Previous pelvic surgery
  • Hypermobility or connective tissue disorders
  • Increased body weight
  • Ageing

Although prolapse is more common after childbirth and during menopause, it can affect women of all ages.

Types of Pelvic Organ Prolapse

Cystocele (Bladder Prolapse)

A cystocele occurs when the bladder drops down and bulges into the front wall of the vagina.

  • A feeling of heaviness or dragging
  • Difficulty emptying the bladder fully
  • Frequent urination
  • Urgency
  • Stress urinary incontinence
  • A visible or noticeable vaginal bulge

Rectocele (Bowel Prolapse)

A rectocele occurs when the rectum bulges into the back wall of the vagina.

  • Constipation
  • Difficulty emptying the bowels
  • Feeling that stool is trapped
  • The need to press around the vagina or perineum to empty the bowel
  • Vaginal pressure or bulging

Uterine Prolapse

Uterine prolapse occurs when the uterus drops lower into the vagina due to weakened support structures.

  • A sensation of heaviness or pulling
  • Low back ache
  • Vaginal pressure
  • Seeing or feeling tissue protruding from the vagina
  • Discomfort during walking, exercise or intimacy

Vault Prolapse

Vault prolapse can occur after a hysterectomy, when the top of the vagina loses support and descends.

Enterocele

An enterocele occurs when part of the small bowel descends into the vaginal space. This is less common but may occur alongside other forms of prolapse.

Prolapse Symptoms

Many women search for prolapse symptoms before realising they may have a pelvic floor issue.

Common symptoms of prolapse include:

  • Vaginal heaviness or dragging
  • A bulge or sensation of “something coming down”
  • Bladder urgency or leakage
  • Difficulty emptying the bladder or bowel
  • Lower back discomfort
  • Pain or discomfort during intimacy
  • Symptoms that worsen after standing, lifting or exercise

Symptoms are often worse at the end of the day or after prolonged periods on your feet.

How is Pelvic Organ Prolapse Diagnosed?

A women’s health physiotherapist or healthcare professional can assess prolapse through:

  • A detailed discussion about symptoms
  • Pelvic floor assessment
  • Vaginal examination
  • Assessment of bladder and bowel function
  • Looking at posture, breathing, lifting habits and exercise routines

The severity of prolapse is often graded from stage 1 to stage 4 depending on how far the prolapse descends.

Pelvic Organ Prolapse Treatment Options

Pelvic Floor Physiotherapy

Pelvic floor muscle training is often the first line of treatment for prolapse.

A specialist women’s health physiotherapist can help with:

  • Teaching correct pelvic floor exercises
  • Improving strength, endurance and coordination
  • Managing pressure through breathing and movement strategies
  • Advice around lifting, exercise and bowel habits
  • Helping women return safely to sport and activity

Pelvic floor rehabilitation can be very effective in reducing symptoms and improving confidence.

Lifestyle Advice

Simple lifestyle changes can help reduce symptoms and prevent prolapse from worsening.

  • Avoiding constipation and straining
  • Managing coughing
  • Modifying heavy lifting
  • Weight management
  • Reducing high-impact exercise if needed
  • Improving posture and breathing patterns

Pessary Fitting for Prolapse

Pessaries are a highly effective non-surgical treatment option for prolapse. Many women search for pessary fitting near me, prolapse support pessary, ring pessary for prolapse or non-surgical prolapse treatment when exploring their options.

Pessaries are medical devices inserted into the vagina to provide support to the pelvic organs.

Pessaries can be an excellent option for women who:

  • Want to avoid or delay surgery
  • Are waiting for surgery
  • Wish to remain active and exercise comfortably
  • Have symptoms that affect work or daily life
  • Are not suitable for surgery
  • Have prolapse during or after pregnancy

There are different types of pessaries available, including:

  • Ring pessaries
  • Cube pessaries
  • Shelf pessaries
  • Gellhorn pessaries
  • Donut pessaries

Benefits of pessaries include:

  • Reduced heaviness and dragging
  • Improved bladder and bowel symptoms
  • Increased comfort during exercise
  • Better quality of life
  • A non-surgical management option

Pessary fitting should always be carried out by a trained healthcare professional. Follow-up appointments are important to ensure the pessary remains comfortable, effective and safe.

When is Surgery Considered?

Surgery may be considered when symptoms are severe or when conservative treatments have not been effective.

There are several surgical procedures available depending on the type of prolapse.

However, surgery is not always the first or only answer. Many women successfully manage their prolapse with physiotherapy, lifestyle changes and pessaries.

You Do Not Have to “Just Put Up With It”

Many women feel embarrassed talking about prolapse, but it is far more common than most people realise. Symptoms such as heaviness, pressure, leakage or vaginal bulging are not something you simply have to live with.

Early assessment and treatment can make a significant difference and help women return to exercise, work and everyday life with confidence.
